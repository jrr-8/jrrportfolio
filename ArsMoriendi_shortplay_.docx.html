Ars Moriendi

HARRIET, 82

BRAM, 21

A graveyard at twilight. BRAM and HARRIET sit on a bench. BRAM is dressed in army fatigues and constantly fidgets. Over the course of the play, the lights dim gradually, mimicking a sunset.

BRAM (pointing): What about that one?

HARRIET: Hmm. He’s visiting his mother. She died of a heart attack. It was sudden, but clean. He’s avoided visiting since the funeral, but he’s finally ready.

BRAM: Wow. (Pointing another direction) And that one?

HARRIET: Grandfather. Dementia. He died in the nursing home, surrounded by family- children, grandchildren, nieces, nephews. A death full of love.

BRAM: How do you do that?

HARRIET (Shrugging): You just make it up. Nothin’ to it.

BRAM: But you don’t even need to think about it. You come up with these whole scenes just like that.

HARRIET: I’ve been doin’ this a while, kid. Got lotsa practice.

BRAM: How long have you been here?

HARRIET: Hmmm. Hard to say. The years all blur together after a bit. Could be forever. Could be not long at all.

BRAM: And you do this a lot? Watch them come and go?

HARRIET: Whole lot.

BRAM: Do you do… anything else?

HARRIET (chuckling): There ain’t much else to do. Death ain’t a cruise ship, boy. There’s no jam-packed schedule of activities to choose from.

BRAM begins jiggling his leg.

You gettin’ antsy there boy?

BRAM: A little.

HARRIET: Get up, stretch your legs. Take a walk.

She sweeps her arms around, indicating the expanse of space.

Plenty of ground to tread. And it’s all yours.

BRAM stands up and begins to walk, but turns back abruptly.

BRAM: Why do I still feel? I shouldn’t be able to get “antsy” without a body, should I?

HARRIET: Why’re you asking me, boy? You think I know how all this works? I got here just the same way as you.

BRAM: Hardly.

HARRIET: Our circumstances may have been a bit different, granted. But after that last moment, we all take the same path. We both ended up here.

BRAM: I guess.

HARRIET: What’s wrong?

BRAM: Nothing.

HARRIET: Well that ain’t true. I may not’ve known you too long but you wear your heart on your sleeve, and your feelings all over your face. What’s wrong?

BRAM (The dam exploding): What isn’t wrong!

HARRIET: Calm down. It ain’t as bad as you think.

BRAM: That’s easy for you to say. You clearly had plenty of time to live. (Pause) Um, no offense?

HARRIET: You think you can insult a dead woman by telling her she looks old?

BRAM shrugs.

You’re a good kid. But you’re too wound up. Don’t worry, you’ve got plenty of time to unwind.

BRAM: I don’t want to unwind! I don’t want to sit here on a bench watching the bereaved come and go, day after day, making up stories about what dead loved one they’re coming to cry over! I want to do things. I want… I want more life.

HARRIET: We all wanted more life. You think I was ready to go? No one’s ready to go. You could knock on every tombstone in this place and ask, and not a single one of ‘em will tell you they were ready to say goodbye to livin’. And if they do, they’re a liar. Like ol’ Gladys.

She jerks her thumb at a particular grave.

Old bitch.

The sound of wind blowing, suddenly loud for a moment. It sounds a little like a woman saying “Fuck you, Harriet!”

Screw you too, Gladys!

HARRIET sticks her tongue out at the grave. BRAM watches this all, perplexed.

BRAM: Is everyone buried here a ghost?

HARRIET: I’m not sure. I never counted.

BRAM: But there are others?

HARRIET: Oh, sure. There’s Gladys, and Seymour, and Fiona, and Isaac, and Sir William—

BRAM: Then how come I can only see you? Shouldn’t there be people everywhere?

HARRIET: I keep tellin’ you, I don’t know how it all works. They didn’t give me a guidebook when I got here, either. You figure it out as you go along. The others’ll start popping up eventually. We all tend to sort of fluctuate in how present we can be. You’re new, so you’re still fulla that life stuff. You could probably go up to one of these breathin’ folks an’ rustle their hair a bit. But you start to settle into things after a while. And then you sorta come and go and such. Not much of a pattern to it. Least as far as I’ve noticed.

BRAM: What does it feel like? When you… settle?

HARRIET: Don’t feel like much at all. Just happens.

BRAM: And then what?

HARRIET: I imagine eventually you stop comin’ and just go. Like I said, time ain’t an exact science here. But, far as I can tell, we don’t got anybody from a real long time ago. They’re gone.

BRAM: Gone where?

HARRIET: You gotta stop asking questions I can’t answer! I don’t know what happens afterwards any more than you do. Any more than I knew that this was waitin’ for me when I snuffed it.

BRAM: Did you believe in an afterlife? When you were… alive?

HARRIET: When I was younger, I reckon. Towards the end, I wasn’t so sure. I still thought there was somethin’, ya know. But not exactly this. And not exactly all that heaven and hell nonsense. What about you?

BRAM: I didn’t believe in anything.

HARRIET: Just one and done?

BRAM: One and done.

HARRIET: Huh.

BRAM: Yeah.

They are silent for a long moment.

HARRIET: Aw, hell. What’s wrong now?

BRAM: Nothing.

HARRIET: Bullshit.

BRAM: I was just… thinking about it.

HARRIET: ‘Bout what?

BRAM: It. The “moment,” or whatever.

HARRIET: Ah. Best not to dwell on that, if ya ask me.

BRAM: I can’t seem to stop. It’s like it’s all I can see.

HARRIET: Show me.

BRAM: What?

HARRIET: Show me. You already told me, but I wanna see it.

BRAM unbuttons the topmost buttons of his uniform and pulls the collar down to reveal several bullet wounds in his chest.

HARRIET: Damn, that’s a nasty one.

BRAM: Yeah.

HARRIET: It hurt? When they shot ya?

BRAM: Oh yeah.

HARRIET: How ‘bout now?

BRAM: Kind of. It’s… distant, though. I can still feel it, but it feels far away. It’s weird. I don’t want to feel it. I would’ve thought the one good thing about dying is it doesn’t hurt anymore.

HARRIET: It’ll pass. Trust me.

She points to a scar on her forehead, the faint remnants of a head wound.

BRAM: That’s yours?

HARRIET: Sure is.

BRAM: It healed?

HARRIET: Not so much. S’more like you forget. And it goes away.

BRAM: How’d you get it?

HARRIET: None of your damn business, boy.

BRAM: Sorry. (Switching tactics) How long did it take? To go away?

HARRIET: How many “time” questions you gonna ask me before you get it?

BRAM: Right. Sorry.

HARRIET: It won’t be too long. At least, I don’t think it was too long for me. It’s not the same for everybody, I reckon. Sir William’s still got his, bloody and shiny as the day they ran that sword through him. But that’s just him. I think he keeps it around on purpose. Likes to show it off.

BRAM: I thought you said there wasn’t anyone here from a long time ago?

HARRIET: I said I didn’t think there was. And I said from a real long time ago. But Sir William’s different. Don’t you worry about Sir William.

BRAM: I’m a little worried. How long ago is Sir William from? How long does it take to pass on?

HARRIET: How long, how long he says! Years, time- you gotta let go of all that, Bram. It takes however long it takes. S’different for everyone. But Sir William sticks. He holds on.

BRAM: He chooses to stay?

HARRIET: Eh, more or less I suppose.

BRAM: Why?

HARRIET: He likes it here. He’s not the only one. S’nice.

BRAM: Do you like it here?

HARRIET: What’s not to like?

BRAM: Plenty. But I meant… Are you…. ‘Holding on?’

HARRIET: So what if I am?

BRAM: Well, don’t you want to move on?

HARRIET: What’s the hurry?

BRAM: This is… well, boring.

HARRIET: Hah! Boring!

BRAM: Yeah.

HARRIET: And you think whatever’s next is gonna be a barrel of laughs?

BRAM: No. I mean, maybe. It could be anything, but anything’s better than just… waiting.

HARRIET: I don’t see it that way.

BRAM: No?

HARRIET: It’s only boring if you let it be, if you’re only focused on what’s to come. Keep trying to speed things up, and they’ll only go slower. I intend to enjoy my time here, however much of it I got.

BRAM: Enjoy it how? People-watching?

HARRIET: What’s wrong with people-watching? You seemed to like it plenty before.

BRAM: Sure, but not for, like, eternity.

HARRIET: It ain’t eternity.

BRAM: I want to do something real.

HARRIET: Real how? What’s that even mean? We’re not real, ff you think about it. Not really here. Not really people. There ain’t no livin’, and there ain’t no doin’. Just bein’.

BRAM: Then how do I… ‘just be?’

HARRIET: You could start by sittin’ down. C’mon, all your pacin’ is makin’ my head spin.

She pats the bench next to her. BRAM sits.

HARRIET: Now tell me, what is it you wanted to do in life?

BRAM: I wanted a lot of things. To see the world. To make my parents proud. To be a hero.

HARRIET: That why you joined the army?

BRAM: Yeah.

HARRIET: All right, let’s try somethin’. I want you to picture everythin’ you wanted to accomplish in life.

BRAM (Closing his eyes): Ok.

HARRIET: All your grand hopes and dreams.

BRAM: All right.

HARRIET: You picturin’ ‘em?

BRAM: Some of them.

HARRIET: Good. Now smash those pictures with a hammer, and tell me what you did for fun.

BRAM: For fun?

HARRIET: Yes, fun. Surely you’ve heard of it. How did you unwind? Relax?

BRAM: I…uh…

HARRIET: C’mon, there’s gotta be somethin’. You’re life can’t all’ve been fightin’ and bleedin’ and dyin’ for your country.

BRAM: I… I don’t know. (Grasping at straws) I used to go running a lot.

HARRIET: Running is the opposite of fun. Next.

BRAM: I don’t know!

HARRIET: C’mon, don’t be dense. You musta had hobbies. Friends, or a sweetheart maybe?

Bram shrugs

Wow. They told me ‘bout people like you, but I never really believed ‘em.

BRAM: People like me?

HARRIET: Ghosts.

BRAM: You’re a ghost.

HARRIET: Yeah, but you were a ghost in life. Just floatin’ through the years, grindin’ away and still never doin’ a lick of nothin’.

BRAM: I did things. I served my country. I had a purpose.

HARRIET: And did you like doin’ that?

BRAM: Yes.

HARRIET: And you were happy?

BRAM: I…

He trails off, the realization finally sinking in.

HARRIET: That’s the thing now, ain’t it? You spent your whole life worryin’ about big things. Makin’ a difference, bein’ all noble. Now I ain’t sayin’ that’s bad. I’m sure you did a lot of good in life. But that was life. Now you’re dead. There’s no more work to be done. You got no purpose to serve. So now you can be miserable, or you can learn to have fun. Let me show you something.

She stands up and leads him over to a grave. The lights shift as they walk, giving the impression of a greater distance traveled in the time it takes to walk across stage.

See that one there? Can you read that epigraph for me?

BRAM (Kneeling down to read): Uh… Fui…non fui…non sum… non curo.

HARRIET: You know what that is?

BRAM: Nope.

HARRIET: It’s Latin.

BRAM (Disbelieving): You know Latin?

HARRIET: Course I do. I’m a genius, didn’t ya know? What’s it mean?

BRAM: How should I know?

HARRIET: Cause they translated it right underneath, you dumbass.

BRAM: Oh. (Reading) “I was. I was not. I am not. I don’t care.” (He turns back to HARRIET) What the fuck?

HARRIET: Somethin’ those old Roman fucks used to write on their graves. ‘Spose whoever this was (she taps the side of the grave with her foot) had a thing for the ancient times. But who cares about him. I just brought you here for the epigraph.

BRAM: What does it mean?

HARRIET: You were. You died. Now you ain’t. So you gotta stop caring.

BRAM: … How?

HARRIET: The fuck should I know? That’s up to you. But I’ll tell you what. I can help you start. Let’s go for a walk. I think you might’ve settled enough by now to see some of the others pop up. Tell me if you spot anyone, and I’ll introduce you.

BRAM (Nervous): I don’t know. I’m not great with people.

HARRIET: Good thing these ain’t people then. C’mon. It’ll be fun.

She holds out a hand, and BRAM accepts it.

 Maybe ol’ Gladys will show herself, and you can see what a fuckin’ ditz she is for yourself.

They begin walking off stage. BLACKOUT.
